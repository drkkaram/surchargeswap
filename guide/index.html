The RBA Surcharge Ban: A Complete Guide for Australian Businesses (2026)

Everything you need to know about October 2026, what it covers, what it doesn't, and your three options.

01

What's actually happening

Following the Reserve Bank of Australia's Payments System Review, the federal government confirmed that card payment surcharges will be banned for Visa, Mastercard, and eftpos transactions effective 1 October 2026. This means businesses will no longer be allowed to pass card processing fees on to customers for these three networks.

The policy aims to reduce costs for consumers who currently pay surcharges averaging 1.1% to 1.5% on card transactions. For businesses, this means the merchant service fee (MSF) becomes a direct operating cost that must be absorbed, offset through payment processor switching, or recovered through price adjustments.

The ban applies to all Australian businesses accepting in-person and online card payments through Visa, Mastercard, and eftpos networks. There is no revenue threshold or small business exemption. (RBA Conclusions Paper, March 2026)

02

Who it affects

An estimated 436,000 Australian businesses currently apply surcharges to card payments (RBA Payments System Board Conclusions Paper, March 2026). The industries most affected include:

  • Hospitality (restaurants, cafes, bars) where margins are already thin
  • Retail with high card transaction volumes
  • Professional services (medical, dental, legal) that typically surcharge
  • Online businesses and e-commerce stores
  • Trades and services using mobile payment terminals

The ban covers three networks: Visa, Mastercard, and eftpos. Two notable exclusions apply: American Express and Buy Now Pay Later services. These are addressed in detail below.

03

The Amex exception explained

American Express is explicitly excluded from the surcharge ban. Businesses can continue to apply surcharges to Amex transactions after 1 October 2026. (As confirmed in the RBA Conclusions Paper, March 2026 — check rba.gov.au for updates.)

This exemption exists because Amex operates as a three-party scheme (issuer, acquirer, and network are all Amex) rather than the four-party model used by Visa, Mastercard, and eftpos. The RBA's interchange regulation only applies to designated four-party schemes.

For many businesses, Amex accounts for 5% to 10% of card volume. The surcharge you collect on Amex transactions post-ban becomes a meaningful offset against the revenue lost from Visa/MC/eftpos surcharges.

Action item: Review your Amex volume and ensure your POS system can apply surcharges selectively by card network.

04

BNPL (Afterpay, Zip) — what the ban doesn't cover

Buy Now Pay Later services including Afterpay, Zip, Klarna, and Humm are not covered by the October 2026 ban. Businesses can continue to surcharge BNPL transactions, though few currently do because BNPL providers typically prohibit it in their merchant agreements.

BNPL is already a significant cost centre for businesses. Typical merchant fees range from 3.5% to 6%, with Afterpay averaging approximately 4.5%. At these rates, BNPL costs your business 3 to 4 times more than standard card processing.

The RBA has flagged BNPL surcharging as an area under active review. No implementation timeline has been announced as of April 2026. For now, BNPL costs remain a separate line item in your payment cost analysis. Check rba.gov.au for updates.

05

Your three options

Every business affected by the ban has three strategic choices:

Option 1: Absorb the cost

Accept the surcharge revenue loss as a new operating expense. Simplest to implement but directly reduces your margin. For a business doing $50,000/month in covered card revenue with a 1.5% surcharge, this means losing $675/month ($8,100/year).

Option 2: Switch processors

Move to a cheaper payment processor to reduce your MSF rate. A drop from 1.7% to 1.2% on $50,000/month saves $250/month ($3,000/year). Combined with the RBA's interchange reduction, savings can be substantial. This requires a one-time effort to switch but delivers ongoing savings.

Option 3: Reprice your products

Increase menu or product prices by your MSF rate (typically 1.5% to 2%). A $20 item becomes $20.34. No switching cost and no admin, but carries risk if competitors hold prices flat. Works best for businesses with price-insensitive customers.

Most businesses will use a combination. The calculator models all three options based on your actual numbers.

06

How to switch payment processors — step by step

  1. Check your current contract. Review your existing processor agreement for lock-in periods, early termination fees, and notice requirements. Most contracts require 30 to 90 days notice.
  2. Compare actual costs. Use the processor comparison or calculator to see real monthly cost differences. Factor in monthly fees, terminal costs, and contract length.
  3. Contact your top 2 choices. Request a formal quote with your specific volume. Published rates are standard; many processors negotiate for higher volumes.
  4. Order equipment early. Terminal delivery and setup can take 1 to 3 weeks. Plan for overlap with your existing terminal so there's no gap in payment acceptance.
  5. Verify settlement timing. Settlement terms vary from same-day (Tyro, Zeller) to T+2 (some banks). Ensure the new settlement schedule works with your cash flow needs.
  6. Cancel your old processor. Send written notice within the contract's required period. Return any rented equipment to avoid ongoing charges.
07

The interchange windfall

The RBA is simultaneously reducing credit card interchange fees from approximately 0.80% to 0.30%. This is separate from the surcharge ban but creates an important opportunity.

Cost-plus processors (like Tyro and most traditional bank merchant facilities) charge you the actual interchange fee plus a fixed margin. When interchange drops, your rate drops automatically. At $50,000/month in Visa/MC volume, a 0.5% interchange reduction saves you $250/month.

Blended flat-rate processors (like Zeller, Square, Stripe) charge a single percentage regardless of interchange. The processor keeps the interchange reduction as additional margin unless they voluntarily lower their published rate.

The question to ask: “Do you use cost-plus pricing or blended flat rate? Will I automatically benefit from the RBA interchange reduction?”

This is the single most important question you can ask your processor before October 2026.

09

Frequently asked questions

Does the ban apply to online businesses?

Yes. The ban covers all Visa, Mastercard, and eftpos transactions whether in-store or online. If you currently add a surcharge at checkout on your website, this must be removed by 1 October 2026.

Does the ban cover prepaid Visa and Mastercard gift cards?

Yes. The RBA confirmed the ban covers debit, prepaid, and credit cards on designated networks. Prepaid Visa and Mastercard gift cards are included. You cannot apply a surcharge to a prepaid card transaction from 1 October 2026.

Can I still surcharge Amex?

Yes. American Express is explicitly excluded from the ban. You can continue to apply surcharges to Amex transactions. Ensure your POS or payment gateway can apply surcharges selectively by card network.

What happens if I keep surcharging after October 2026?

The ACCC will enforce the ban. Penalties for non-compliance include fines and infringement notices. Consumers can lodge complaints directly with the ACCC. See our enforcement FAQ for details.

Will the ban reduce my processing fees?

Not directly. The surcharge ban prevents you from passing fees to customers. However, the RBA is separately reducing interchange fees, which will lower processing costs for businesses on cost-plus pricing.

Is there a small business exemption?

No. As confirmed in the RBA Conclusions Paper (March 2026), the ban applies to all businesses regardless of size or revenue. There is no threshold or exemption for small businesses. Check rba.gov.au for any updates before October 2026.

What about Afterpay and Zip surcharges?

BNPL services are not covered by the October 2026 ban. However, most BNPL provider agreements already prohibit merchants from surcharging. Check your BNPL merchant agreement for specific terms.

How much will the interchange reduction save me?

If you use a cost-plus processor, you'll benefit from approximately 0.5% reduction on credit card interchange. On $50,000/month in Visa/MC volume, that's roughly $250/month. Flat-rate processors may not pass on this saving.

Should I switch processors or just absorb the cost?

It depends on your volume and current rate. For most businesses doing over $20,000/month in card revenue, switching to a cheaper processor delivers measurable savings. Use the calculator to compare your specific numbers.

How long does it take to switch processors?

Typically 2 to 6 weeks from decision to live transactions. This includes contract review (1 week), application and approval (3 to 5 days), equipment delivery (1 to 2 weeks), and setup/testing (1 to 2 days).

Can I negotiate a lower rate with my current processor?

Yes. Many processors will negotiate, especially if you can show a competitive quote. Contact your current provider with a formal quote from a competitor and ask them to match or beat it. The worst they can say is no.
